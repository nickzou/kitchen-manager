Founder readiness checklist

Kitchen Manager · Draft

Drafted2026-05-30
PurposeA landscape of "what else you need" beyond the technical roadmap and GTM strategy — to refer back to when the universe of unknowns feels infinite.

Read this first

A little fear at this stage is the right signal. Almost everyone who ships a real product is scared right before launch. It's also worth saying out loud that you're starting from a meaningfully better position than most first-time founders:

  • You have a working product. Most people at "thinking about launching" stage don't.
  • You've dogfooded it for months on real data, which is rare and valuable.
  • You have a day job covering rent — you're not panic-launching.
  • You've thought clearly about what you do and don't want (no VC, charge upfront, no nickel-and-diming, no data farming, no growth-at-all-costs). Most founders never name those, and end up reactively building something they hate.
  • You have detailed technical and GTM strategy docs already, which puts you ahead of probably 80% of solo founders at this stage.

The thing first-timers underestimate isn't usually how much there is to do. It's how much of the work is psychological — the willingness to be in public, to charge for something, to email a stranger and say "would you buy this," to keep going when the metrics are flat for the first three months. The Phase 0–4 technical work is the easy part. You already know how to do that.

What's still missing — ranked by when you need it

None of these is large. Most are 1–2 pages. The point of this document is to make the universe of unknowns finite, so it stops feeling infinite.

Critical before Phase 1 · closed beta

Three pieces that unblock everything downstream

  • Brand identity decision. Name, wordmark, type pair, basic visual language. Blocks the marketing site, the landing page, ads, business name registration, everything. Worth a focused weekend.
  • Customer interview guide + first 5 calls. The mechanics of "who do I ask, what do I ask them, how do I write it up." Otherwise the calls become rambling chats and you don't learn anything actionable. These calls will reshape every other doc.
  • Personal financial runway plan. A spreadsheet that says: "I have $X saved, my burn is $Y/mo, my day job covers everything → runway is forever. If I quit the day job at MRR $Z, runway becomes N months." Background anxiety drops once this is written down.

Critical before Phase 2 · open beta

Things you need once strangers are in the door

  • Terms of Service + Privacy Policy + Cookie consent. Legal floor. Generated via iubenda or Termly to start; lawyer pass before Phase 3 paid launch.
  • Customer support playbook. Where tickets go, response-time commitment, what canned responses you have, when you escalate "this is a bug" to "this is a code change." Two pages is enough.
  • Help docs / FAQ. Stops support tickets at the source. Even 5 well-written pages help. The value-conscious ICP especially needs self-serve help — they didn't sign up to email a developer.
  • Brand voice guide. Two pages: how we talk vs. how we don't. Saves you from rewriting every email and ad in a different tone.

Critical before Phase 3 · paid launch

Things you need before taking real money

  • Refund policy. Written, on the marketing site, generous (14-day no-questions is the default; the trust signal is worth more than the few refund requests it generates).
  • Incident response runbook. What you do when prod is down at 2am. Mostly so you don't have to think. Cover: auth down, payments down, DB down, host down, you're sick.
  • Risk register. One-pager of "things that could kill this and how I'd respond." Examples: hosting bill explodes, a competitor clones you, day-job politics shift, partner/family needs your time, health event. Naming risks reduces their psychological weight.
  • Sales tax + bookkeeping cadence. Monthly check-in: what gets recorded where in Wave, when invoices are reconciled, when you check the HST threshold. Half an hour a month if you set it up properly.

Useful · easy to defer

Things worth having eventually, not urgent

  • Product north star. "If this works wildly in 5 years, what does it look like?" Useful for resisting feature creep. Easy to skip for now.
  • Decision log. "Why did I do X" written down as you go. Useful in month 18 when you've forgotten. Can be informal — a single markdown file.
  • Press kit. Boilerplate company description, logo files, screenshots, founder quote. Phase 3+ thing.
  • Email cadence / content calendar. When and what you send. Phase 2+ once you have a list to send to.

Personal · the part nobody talks about

Things that keep you running

  • Time-budget plan. Which evenings, which weekend hours. When you don't work on this. Without it you'll work every spare hour and resent the project by month 4. Indie founders burn out faster from "always on" than from a single bad sprint.
  • Quit-day-job criteria. Already in the GTM blindspots section. Worth pulling into its own one-pager. Numerically defined: at what MRR / lifetime sales would you reduce day-job hours? At what number would you quit entirely?
  • Sustainability check-ins. Monthly. Five questions: am I still enjoying this? Am I sleeping? Is my partner okay with where time is going? Am I learning? Would I want to be doing this in 12 months? Indie projects die from burnout more often than from bad strategy. The check-in is the early warning system.

What's not on this list (and why)

  • Pitch deck. Not raising VC. Don't make one. If a partnership conversation comes up later that needs slides, a 6-slide overview deck is plenty.
  • Board / advisors. Solo, not raising. Don't add structure you don't need.
  • Detailed financial projections. At this scale they're fiction. Track actuals, project one month ahead, not a year.
  • Sales playbook in the B2B sense. Wrong shape for a $36/yr B2C product. The funnel is the marketing site and onboarding.

Suggested order

Pick one thing this week, not five. The bias for first-time founders is over-preparation as procrastination. The customer interview guide is my pick because the calls will reshape every other doc and you can't really plan further without that input. Brand identity is a close second if you want to feel forward momentum.

Or: don't draft anything this week. Sit with the list. Talk to your partner about runway. Go for a walk. The work will be there.

The biggest risk at this stage isn't that you'll miss a category on this list. It's over-preparation as procrastination. At some point you have to talk to strangers and let them poke at your thing. Everything in this doc is in service of that, not a substitute for it.
